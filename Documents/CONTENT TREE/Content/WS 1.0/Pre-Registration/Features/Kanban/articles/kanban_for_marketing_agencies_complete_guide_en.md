# Kanban for Marketing Agencies: The Complete Guide to Managing Client Projects

Running a marketing agency means juggling multiple clients, campaigns, and deadlines simultaneously. When tasks are scattered across email threads, chat messages, and spreadsheets, things get chaotic. Deadlines slip. Team members don't know who's working on what. And clients start asking uncomfortable questions about project progress.

This guide shows you how Kanban methodology helps marketing agencies take control of their workflow, improve visibility, and deliver projects on time.

## What is Kanban and Why Marketing Agencies Need It

### The Basics of Kanban

Kanban is a visual project management method that originated in Toyota's manufacturing plants in the 1940s. The name comes from Japanese words meaning "visual card" or "signboard."

The core concept is simple: visualize your work on a board with columns representing different stages. Tasks move from left to right as they progress through your workflow. A typical Kanban board might have columns like "Backlog," "In Progress," "Review," and "Done."

What makes Kanban powerful for marketing teams:

- **Visual clarity** — Everyone sees the status of every task at a glance
- **Work-in-progress (WIP) limits** — Prevent team overload by limiting active tasks
- **Flow management** — Identify and eliminate bottlenecks quickly
- **Flexibility** — Adapt to changing priorities without disrupting the entire system

### Why Traditional Project Management Fails for Agencies

Marketing agencies face unique challenges that traditional project management approaches struggle to address:

**Multiple concurrent projects.** Unlike product companies that might focus on one or two major initiatives, agencies typically manage 10-50 active client projects simultaneously. Traditional timeline-based planning breaks down when everything happens at once.

**Unpredictable client requests.** Clients change their minds. Campaigns need adjustments. Urgent requests appear without warning. Rigid project plans can't accommodate this reality.

**Cross-functional collaboration.** A single campaign might involve strategists, copywriters, designers, developers, and account managers. Without clear visibility, work falls through the cracks.

**Deadline pressure.** Marketing campaigns often have immovable launch dates tied to events, seasons, or business milestones. Missing deadlines costs money and damages client relationships.

As one agency founder told us: "We were losing tasks, and it was costing us money. Email simply doesn't work for project management."

### Key Principles: Visualize, Limit WIP, Manage Flow

**Principle 1: Visualize Work**

When you can see all work in one place, you can make better decisions. A Kanban board shows:
- What's waiting to start
- What's actively being worked on
- Where work is stuck
- What's completed

This transparency eliminates the constant "what's the status of...?" questions that waste everyone's time.

**Principle 2: Limit Work in Progress**

The biggest mistake teams make? Taking on too much at once. When everyone has 15 tasks "in progress," nothing actually moves forward.

WIP limits force focus. If your design column has a WIP limit of 3, no new design tasks can start until one finishes. This might feel restrictive initially, but it dramatically improves completion rates.

**Principle 3: Manage Flow**

Kanban encourages continuous improvement by making bottlenecks visible. If tasks consistently pile up in the "Client Review" column, you know where to focus your optimization efforts.

## Setting Up Your Marketing Agency Kanban Board

### Choosing Your Columns (Backlog → Done)

The right column structure depends on your agency's workflow. Here's a proven starting point for marketing agencies:

**Basic Structure:**
1. **Backlog** — All incoming requests and planned work
2. **Ready** — Prioritized and ready to start
3. **In Progress** — Actively being worked on
4. **Review** — Internal quality check
5. **Client Approval** — Waiting for client feedback
6. **Done** — Completed and delivered

**Advanced Structure for Larger Teams:**
1. **Ideas/Backlog**
2. **This Sprint** — Work planned for current period
3. **Content Creation**
4. **Design**
5. **Internal Review**
6. **Client Review**
7. **Revisions**
8. **Ready to Publish**
9. **Published/Done**

Avoid creating too many columns initially. Start simple and add columns only when you identify specific workflow stages that need visibility.

### Creating Swimlanes for Client Projects

Swimlanes are horizontal divisions that help organize work by category. For agencies, the most useful swimlane approaches:

**By Client:**
Each major client gets their own swimlane. This makes it easy to see all work for a specific client and prevents one client's urgent requests from burying another client's tasks.

**By Campaign Type:**
- Social Media
- Content Marketing
- Paid Advertising
- Email Marketing
- Website Projects

**By Priority:**
- Urgent
- High Priority
- Standard
- Low Priority

Marketing Office, a Ukrainian agency managing 50+ clients, uses client-based swimlanes combined with a project Kanban view. Their result? Zero missed deadlines despite handling hundreds of concurrent tasks.

### Setting WIP Limits for Each Stage

WIP limits prevent overload and improve flow. Here are recommended starting points for marketing teams:

| Stage | Suggested WIP Limit | Reasoning |
|-------|---------------------|-----------|
| In Progress | 2-3 per person | Focus on completion over starting |
| Design | Team size × 1.5 | Account for revision cycles |
| Review | 5 total | Don't let reviews become a bottleneck |
| Client Approval | No limit* | You can't control client response time |

*While you can't limit client approval, you can set policies like automatic follow-ups after 48 hours.

**How to determine your WIP limits:**

1. Track your current flow for 2 weeks without limits
2. Identify where tasks pile up (bottlenecks)
3. Set limits slightly below your current average
4. Adjust based on results

Remember: WIP limits should feel slightly uncomfortable. If they're easy to maintain, they're probably too high.

### Adding Labels, Tags, and Due Dates

Effective labeling makes your Kanban board more useful:

**Essential Labels for Agencies:**
- **Client name** — Always know who the work is for
- **Project type** — Content, design, development, strategy
- **Priority** — Critical, high, medium, low
- **Due date** — Never miss a deadline

**Optional Labels:**
- Campaign name
- Assigned team member
- Estimated hours
- Billable/non-billable

**Due Date Best Practices:**
- Set due dates relative to client deadlines (leave buffer time)
- Use color coding for urgency (red for overdue, yellow for due soon)
- Include time zones for international clients

## 5 Steps to Implement Kanban in Your Agency

### Step 1: Map Your Current Workflow

Before creating your board, understand how work actually flows through your agency:

**Document your process:**
1. List all stages a typical project goes through
2. Identify who is involved at each stage
3. Note where handoffs occur
4. Find where work typically gets stuck

**Questions to answer:**
- How do new requests enter the system?
- Who decides what gets worked on next?
- What triggers movement from one stage to the next?
- Where do delays most often occur?

**Pro tip:** Walk through 3-5 recent projects and track exactly what happened at each stage. Reality often differs from how we think things work.

### Step 2: Create Your Initial Board

Start simple. You can always add complexity later.

**Initial setup checklist:**
- [ ] Choose your tool (Worksection, Trello, physical board)
- [ ] Create 4-6 columns based on your workflow mapping
- [ ] Add all current active tasks
- [ ] Assign owners to each task
- [ ] Add due dates where known

**For physical boards:** Use sticky notes on a whiteboard. Different colors for different clients or task types.

**For digital boards:** Most teams find digital boards more practical because:
- Remote team members can access them
- Cards can contain detailed information
- History and changes are tracked
- Integrations automate updates

Worksection provides a built-in Kanban view that requires no additional setup. Tasks automatically appear on the board based on their status.

### Step 3: Set WIP Limits

Don't skip this step. WIP limits are what transform a task board into a Kanban system.

**Start conservatively:**
- Begin with limits that feel achievable
- Track how often limits are hit
- Tighten limits gradually as the team adapts

**Team discussion points:**
- What happens when we hit a WIP limit?
- Who can override limits, and when?
- How do we handle urgent client requests?

**Common mistake:** Setting WIP limits too high. If you never hit your limits, they're not doing anything.

### Step 4: Run Daily Standups

Short daily meetings keep the board current and the team aligned:

**Standup format (15 minutes max):**
1. Walk the board from right to left (focus on completing work)
2. For each column, discuss blocked items first
3. Identify any cards without recent updates
4. Note any WIP limit concerns

**Key questions:**
- What will be completed today?
- What's blocking progress?
- Does anyone need help?

**Avoid:** Long discussions about specific tasks. Take those offline.

### Step 5: Review and Optimize Weekly

Continuous improvement is central to Kanban:

**Weekly review agenda:**
1. **Flow metrics** — Average time in each column
2. **Blockers** — What caused delays this week?
3. **WIP analysis** — Were limits respected? Should they change?
4. **Process improvements** — What small change could help?

**Monthly retrospective questions:**
- Which column has the longest average time?
- What types of tasks get stuck most often?
- Are our WIP limits still appropriate?
- What would make our flow smoother?

## Kanban vs Scrum for Marketing Teams

| Aspect | Kanban | Scrum |
|--------|--------|-------|
| Planning | Continuous | Sprint-based (1-4 weeks) |
| Roles | No required roles | Scrum Master, Product Owner |
| Changes | Welcome anytime | Wait for next sprint |
| Meetings | Daily standup | Daily, plus planning, review, retro |
| Work Units | Cards/tasks | User stories |
| Metrics | Lead time, throughput | Velocity |
| Best For | Ongoing work, service teams | Project-based work, product teams |

### When to Use Kanban

Kanban works best when:
- Work arrives unpredictably (client requests)
- You need flexibility to change priorities quickly
- Projects have continuous delivery rather than fixed releases
- Your team handles multiple small tasks rather than large projects
- You're new to agile and want a gentle starting point

**Marketing agency scenarios perfect for Kanban:**
- Social media management
- Ongoing content creation
- Support and maintenance retainers
- Ad campaign optimization
- PR and communications

### When to Use Scrum

Consider Scrum when:
- Projects have clear beginning and end dates
- Work can be planned in 2-4 week chunks
- The team is dedicated to fewer, larger projects
- Stakeholders need predictable delivery schedules

**Marketing scenarios suited to Scrum:**
- Major website redesigns
- Product launches with fixed dates
- Large campaign development
- Brand identity projects

### Hybrid Approaches

Many agencies use both methodologies:

**Scrumban approach:**
- Use sprints for planning and retrospectives
- Use Kanban board for daily work visualization
- Keep WIP limits from Kanban
- Maintain sprint goals from Scrum

## Common Kanban Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

### No WIP Limits

**The problem:** Without WIP limits, your Kanban board is just a task list with columns. Work piles up everywhere, and the team gets overwhelmed.

**The fix:**
- Start with WIP limits from day one
- Make limits visible on the board
- Create clear policies for when limits can be exceeded
- Review and adjust limits monthly

### Too Many Columns

**The problem:** Fifteen columns might seem detailed, but they create confusion. Tasks get stuck between columns, and the board becomes hard to read.

**The fix:**
- Start with 5-6 columns maximum
- Each column should represent meaningful work
- Combine stages that always happen together
- Add columns only when you identify specific bottlenecks

### Ignoring Bottlenecks

**The problem:** When tasks consistently pile up in one column, it indicates a systemic issue. Ignoring it means the problem gets worse.

**The fix:**
- Review flow metrics weekly
- Investigate any column with growing backlog
- Consider: staffing, process, or dependency issues
- Make bottleneck resolution a priority

### Not Updating the Board

**The problem:** A Kanban board is only useful if it reflects reality. Stale data leads to bad decisions and erodes team trust in the system.

**The fix:**
- Make board updates part of daily routine
- Review board status at standup meetings
- Set expectations that updates happen immediately
- Consider integrations that automate status changes

## FAQ — Frequently Asked Questions

### What is Kanban in marketing?

Kanban in marketing is a visual workflow management method where marketing tasks and campaigns are represented as cards on a board. Cards move through columns like "Backlog," "In Progress," "Review," and "Done." This gives marketing teams clear visibility into all work, helps prevent overload, and makes it easy to track campaign progress.

### How do I set WIP limits for my marketing team?

Start by tracking your current workflow for 1-2 weeks without limits. Note where tasks pile up and how many items team members typically have in progress. Set initial limits slightly below your observed average — for example, if designers usually have 4-5 items in progress, start with a limit of 3. Adjust based on flow improvement over the following weeks.

### Kanban vs Scrum: Which is better for marketing agencies?

For most marketing agencies, Kanban is more practical because agency work is typically ongoing and unpredictable. Client requests arrive constantly, priorities shift, and campaigns require continuous optimization rather than fixed releases. Scrum works better for project-based work with clear deliverables and dates, like website redesigns or major product launches. Many agencies successfully use a hybrid "Scrumban" approach.

### How do I handle urgent client requests in a Kanban system?

Options include: a dedicated "Urgent" swimlane with its own WIP limit, an expedite ticket type that can bypass normal WIP limits, or a rule that urgent requests require dropping another task. The key is having a defined process rather than letting everything become urgent.
